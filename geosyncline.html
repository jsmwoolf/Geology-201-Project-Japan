Geosyncline

Even though the term is obselete in the geological world, the details described on this website strikes controversy in Japanese geology.  Geosyncline is the definition used when a linear trough of subsidence of the Earth's crust within which vast amounts of sediments accumulate.  As argued by Kobayashi Teiichi, deceased professor at the University of Tokyo and proposal of geosyncline in Japan's formation, the mountain chains were formed by foldings of the surface.  In the 70s, however, researchers such as Uyeda Seiya and Miyashiro Akiho applied plate tectonics in the formation of Japan.  

Despite the plate tectonic theory being accepted in the west this time, Japanese geologist rejected the hypothesis.  Thus, a battle between the two idealogies ensured.  Initially, the idea of plate tectonics was dismissed in favor of geosyncline.  In the 80s and 90s, however, several findings gave the hypothesis more substance:

  • Refinements in biostratigraphy based on microfossils
  • Refinements in dating weakly metamorphosed strata
  • Acceptance of the concept of linked subduction and accretion
  • Development of palaeomagnetism as a measure of geographical location.

Eventually, the idea of plate tectonics started to become incorporated in Japanese schoolbooks.  Unfortunately, the idea is often mashed together with geosyncline, thus creating confusion on how Japan actually formed.
