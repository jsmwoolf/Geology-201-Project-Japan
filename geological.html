Geological Sequence

Precambrian Cambrian Ordovician Silurian Pennysylvanian Permian Triassic Jurassic Cretaceous Cenozoic

Despite being non-recognizable, the oldest rocks are metamorphic rocks that date back from 1.3 to 1 billion years old, by the Late Proterozoic.  The rocks would have belonged to a supercontinent called Rodinia.  About 750-600 million years ago, Rodinia brokeoff when the proto-Pacific Basin was formed.  The continental blocks of Sino-Korea and Yangtze were formed.  At this time, the cratons were passive rift margins.

Geology of Japan

About 500 million years ago, the edges of the Sino-Korea and Yangtze became active subduction margins.  The subduction processes allowed the cratons to become part of the Japanese landmass.

About 450 million years ago, the subduction between two oceanic plates into the off-shore trench.  This allowed igneous rock to form from the superheated magma from below.

Volcanic features originating from a superplume were found in the Pathalassa Sea near the Australian continent.  Between this time and the Permian, then features would travel across the proto-Pacific basin.  These features would eventually be found in the Akiyoshi and Maizuru Belts.

Location of the belts

Several of the high-pressure metamorphosed accretionary complexes was exhumed to form the Renge Belt.  The belt formed about 300 million years ago and consist of schists.  It is the third oldest geological belt.

The Farallon plate got subducted by the Yangtze craton.  The subduction resulted in the creation of the Akiyoshi, Maizuru, and Sangun Belts from sediments onto the continental margin.  The belts were accretioned to the edge of the Yangtze craton.

The Sino-Korea and Yangtze collided with each other in polar direction.  This ended up closing the paleo-Tethys seaway.  The collision is represented near the Qinling-Dabie mountainous zone in China.  The Hida and Hitachi-Takanuki belts were exhumed metamorphic belts that formed at the eastern expansion of the collision.  At the end of this time period, the oceanic ridge between the Farallon and Izanagi Plates moved northwards with the plates.  This caused the exhumation of the Sangun metamorphic belt.

Location of the Yangtze craton today

Around this time, Pangea begins to break up.  This causes the extension of Tethys Sea ridge to split off, forming the northern (Laurasia) and southern (Gondwana) continental mass.  The proto-Japan area experienced the accreting of the oceanic floor sediments and the Akasaka-Kuzuu.  During this time, the Mino-Tanba, Ryouke Chichibu, and N. Kitakami-Oushima accertionary complexes accounted for nearly 50% of the current archipelagic basement.

The Mikabu and Sorachi plateaus were incorporated into the proto-Japnese landmass.  During this time, the Izanagi Plate changed its direction to be more northerly.  This change on the plate produced two results.  The first result allowed the accretionary complexes formed in the Jurassic to be more in line with modern day Japan.  The other result was that accretionary and igneous activity were halted.

The change, however, was not permanent as the Izanagi Plate changed directions again.  This ended up increasing accertionary activity in the Ryouke Belt, eventually creating the north Shimanto Belt.  During this time period, Sanbagawa Belt was exhumed gradually from south to north.  Finally, a new plate formed off the northern edge of the Laurasian continent, containing several landmass that would become the Kamachatka Peninsula.

Biological:

Dipiction of Fukuivenator

There were several fossils that originate from the Cretaceous:

  • Phosphorosaurus ponpetelegans
  • Fukuiraptor kitadanensis
  • Fukuititan nipponensis (see above)
  • Fukuivenator paradoxus
  • Theropoda
  • Sauropods

Some of these fossils were found in the Kitadani Formation in Fukui, Japan.

Japan underwent several structural modifications during the Cenozoic period.  These modifications eventually became to be known as modern Japan.

First, during the Eocene, was the formation of the oceanic Philippine Plate and the Izu Arc.  In the Miocene, upwelling under the eastern Eurasian continent caused rifts in the continetal crust.  These rifts eventually formed the Lake Baikal and Bohai Basin.  However, the Japan and Kurile Sea basins split apart and the basin floor flooded with magma, creating oceanic-type crust.  This created the Japanese and Kurile archipelagos.  

Around this time, the two portions of Japan split off from the continent and created a "double door" effect, opening the Japan Sea Basin.  The sea eventually stopped opening further due to encountering the Ize Arc.  The earliest dated and westernmost geological belts, Hida and Oki, formed.

Additionally, the Median Tectonic Line (MTL) and the Tanakura Tectonic Line (TTL) originated around this time period.  The MTL runs east-west through southwestern Japan and TTL runs north-south in northeastern Japan.  Fragments of the Hida and Oki belts were moved into the Touhoku region, positioning the Hitachi-Takanuki and the Southern Kitakami Belts.

Finally, the fragments of Hokkaido aligned with Japan, occurring about 15 million years ago.  This becomes the recognizable Japan we know of today.

Biological:

The Dainichi Formation contains some molluscan fossils. The formation dates back from the Pliocene.

The Iwaki Formation is known to have three mammalian fossil families:

  • Anthracotheriidae - Hippopotamus-like animals.
  • Entelodontidae - Known as hell pigs.
  • Ruminantia - Contains the Hypertragulidae
